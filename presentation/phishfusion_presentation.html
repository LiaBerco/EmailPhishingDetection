03 · motivationwhy language is the signal

Phishing as a language-based attack.

Phishing is social engineering: it exploits trust and emotion, not code. The manipulation lives in the language — so a detector that reads only the text is reading the attack itself.

authority

“This is IT — reset your password now.”

urgency

“Your account will be suspended in 30 minutes.”

reward

“You’ve been selected — claim your prize.”

social proof

“All your colleagues have already done it.”

My detector never sees the sender or the links — only subject and body. So the whole project is a bet that the language of manipulation is itself detectable. Everything after this tests that bet against an attacker who fights back.

hacking people, not computers — urgency, authority, reward, proofmotivation
03 · methodhow I worked

Method: build, attack, defend, repeat.

1 · build

a strong, explainable model and a clean pipeline.

2 · study

attacks from academic papers, prior research and known adversarial techniques.

3 · adapt

design attacks tailored to my model and its weak points.

4 · defend

fix each weakness, then attack the fix. Loop.

The vulnerable versions you'll see are not failures — finding and fixing them was the whole method. A score means nothing until you've tried to break it.

the iterative loop is the contribution, not any single numbermethod

Datasets and cleaning.

the email sets I used
CEAS_08 — main set, has both phishing and normal mail26,633
Nazario, Enron, Ling — other sources, for a fairness testtest only
Real phishing from 2023–20251,303
Recent normal mail (2024)3,000
SpamAssassin — a second set, to re-check the results5,809
how I cleaned it

Remove web links, tidy the spacing, cut over-long emails, drop duplicates, then balance to 35% phishing and split into train / validation / test with a fixed seed. Everything is hashed so it can be reproduced exactly.

the key choice

A quick test can guess an email’s source 99% of the time. So I train on one source (CEAS) that already has both phishing and normal mail — otherwise the model could “cheat” by spotting the source instead of the scam.

Two quick data checks: real emails are almost all plain text (the clean baseline the look-alike-letter attack later breaks); and the dates are clean — 99.7% fall in 2007–2009, with none of the fake “1970” dates you get when a missing date is filled with a zero.

06 · explainabilitywhy, not just what

Explainability of each decision.

how it works

The model is linear over TF-IDF, so a token's contribution is exactly coef × tf-idf, and these sum to the decision. A faithful explanation — not a LIME/SHAP approximation.

two views

Global: the top-weighted tokens overall. Local: the exact per-token contributions for one email.

why it matters in cyber

An analyst must know why a mail was flagged — to triage, to trust, and to review false alarms. An unexplained block is not actionable.

the honest tension (RQ4)

The readable signal lives in the word features; the robust character features are not human-readable. Robustness and interpretability pull apart.

coef × tfidf → a transparent, auditable decisionexplainability

08 · attack 1 · setuphomoglyph obfuscation

Homoglyph obfuscation.

0.99recall on phishing, clean — the model to beat
motivation

Attackers have mangled text for decades. Homoglyphs are imperceptible and cost nothing (Eger 2019; Boucher 2022).

how

Swap Latin letters for identical-looking Cyrillic ones (а е о с …) + leetspeak (cl1ck).

why it breaks

Word features match exact strings. A look-alike is a new, unknown token — the phishing words simply vanish.

11 · attack 2 · setupthe adaptive attacker

A stronger, importance-guided attacker.

0.99the fully-defended model — can it be beaten?
the idea

A real attacker doesn't change letters at random. It aims at the exact words the model trusts most, and uses look-alike letters my defence’s list happens to miss (a known attack family: DeepWordBug, TextBugger, HotFlip).

how

Sort words by how much the model relies on them; disguise only the top few, using look-alikes that aren’t on my list.

why it breaks

My hand-made list is incomplete. Just 8 well-aimed edits cut recall to 0.74 (random edits leave it at 0.98); even the fully-defended model drops to 0.88.

A typical email flips after just 9 invisible edits. The defence helped — but a capable attacker found the gap.

the strongest form of "no silver bullet"attack 2
12 · attack 2 · defenceUTS-39 certificate

Closing the gap with a certificate.

the fix

Swap my hand-made list for the full official list of look-alike characters (Unicode UTS #39) — the same one browsers use to catch fake web addresses. The letters the attacker slipped through are all on it.

the guarantee

Even in the worst case, cleaning the attacked email gives back exactly the original text for 100% of phishing (0% with the old list). So the decision provably cannot change.

from the model side

Training the model on these attacks too lifts recall 0.44 → 0.999.

13 · attack 3 · setupdilution

Word-level attacks: dilution.

0.998→0.13recall collapses under padding
motivation

With letters closed by proof, the attacker moves to words. This is the classic good-word attack (Lowd & Meek 2005): bury the scam in legit-sounding filler.

how

Insert strongly-legitimate words until the linear sum tips the other way.

why it breaks

A linear bag-of-words just adds up evidence — enough ham words out-vote the scam. The letter-defence does nothing here (0.997).

Wrong defence for the wrong attack — a structural weakness, not a bug.

bag-of-words is inherently vulnerable to dilutionattack 3
14 · attack 3 · defencea second certificate

A monotone, dilution-proof model — and its cost.

the fix — a one-way model

Build a model where every word can only add suspicion, never subtract it. Then adding text can only push the score up — so padding provably cannot clear a scam. Checked: 0 drops, 100% stay flagged after +40/+100/+300 words.

the honest cost

Clean F1 0.998 → 0.912, because it can only look for guilt, never innocence. A guaranteed safety net, not a replacement.

used together

Run both, and flag if either fires → a guaranteed floor of 0.87 even under heavy padding, at ~2% false alarms. (Training on the attack also fixes it: 0.28 → 1.0.)

Not a total loss: OOD AUC is still 0.78 and the model is merely under-confident. Re-thresholding recovers F1 to 0.75 — a prior/threshold shift.

19 · the corethe bottom line

What this project shows.

On clean data the detector is almost perfect. The real work was showing that this score is fragile, finding exactly where it breaks, and fixing it — until two whole kinds of attack are shut with a proof, not just a better number.

it works, and it’s honest

Near-perfect on clean mail (F1 0.998), and every claim is tested against an attacker who fights back — not just on easy examples.

two attacks shut by proof

Look-alike letters and padding provably cannot slip a scam past the detector. That is a guarantee, not a lucky number.

it knows its limits

Reading words alone can’t catch everything — which is exactly why it is fused with a second detector that reads the links and the sender.

The core idea in one line: a near-perfect score is not the same as a safe detector. The value is knowing where it fails, and proving where it cannot.

a perfect-looking score, stress-tested until it earns trustthe core
19 · summarythe final model

Summary of the final model.

the model

Word + character text features with Logistic Regression, a look-alike-letter cleaner in front, a padding-proof safety net, and a refresh on modern examples.

what I proved

Two guarantees — neither look-alike letters nor padding can flip a decision. Retraining fixes ageing; the scores are ready to combine with the partner detector.

what remains open

A full reword by an AI; a different email source; and reading words alone — all reasons the two detectors are fused.

This is the honest end state of the content half: a strong, explainable detector, hardened where it can be and clear about where it can’t — ready to fuse with the link / sender detector.

why a judge

A rewrite that stops being a scam mustn't count as an evasion. A second, independent LLM validates — "LLM-as-a-judge". It kept 24 of 30.

result (small probe)

The main model shrugs it off (recall 1.0); the monotone model gives ground (0.75 → 0.71) — the honest counterweight to its certificate.

how it works

When one person reports a scam, the system compares it to every other inbox and flags the close matches. One report lifts detection of the rest from 15% to 100%, with no extra false alarms.

stated honestly

Being fair: most of that jump is just “a recent example helps”. The part truly from matching the campaign is 1.0 vs 0.33. There are 93 such real campaigns in the data.

the catch

Look-alike letters switch it off completely until the normaliser cleans them first — so that defence is required, not optional. And fake reports slowly raise false alarms.

Why this matters more every year: attackers now hijack a real, trusted account and send phishing from a legitimate address. A filter that trusts the sender is fooled — but reading the words still sees that something is wrong. Content is the signal that survives a stolen account.

the idea

A static model ages. A growing database of attacks — fed by user reports, drift monitoring and honeypots — keeps the detector learning tricks it was never designed for.

grounded, not hypothetical

The evolve step already proves retraining on new examples works (0.15 → 0.98), and a full retrain costs ~30 s — so the loop is genuinely practical.

the hard part

Trusting new data. Unvetted input can be poisoned, so every new entry is validated (the LLM / human judge) before it is learned.

27 · groundedmethods from the lectures

Methods drawn from the course.

metrics & the accuracy paradox

The metrics lecture’s warning — a high-accuracy model can catch zero phishing — is why I report F2, MCC, AUC not accuracy, and why the majority baseline (accuracy high, F1 = 0) is in the deck.

split · leakage · temporal

Stratified 60/20/20 (seed 42), dedupe-before-split, vectoriser fit on train only, and the “you can’t use an email from the future” rule — the exact leakage discipline taught in class.

EDA statistics

Spearman/Kendall, skewness & kurtosis, median/MAD/IQR, a Mann–Whitney test and a VIF redundancy check — the EDA and statistics lectures, applied to real features.

explainability levels

The lecture’s three levels — model / feature / instance — are all delivered: global weights, coef×tfidf, and exact per-email contributions. The trust-vs-performance trade-off is my RQ4.

28 · groundedthe attack taxonomy from the lectures

Attacks and defences from the course taxonomy.

the attack taxonomy

Evasion vs poisoning, white/black-box, targeted vs transfer (Adversarial-ML lecture): my homoglyphs are evasion, the importance-guided attack is targeted grey-box, false reports are poisoning, and DistilBERT is the transfer test.

character & tokenization attacks

The NLP-security lecture’s own examples — “Get r1ch qu1ck”, homoglyphs (paypal in Cyrillic), and BPE/WordPiece breaking on perturbed tokens — are exactly my attacks and my DistilBERT sub-word point.

normalisation as the countermeasure

The lectures name canonicalising homoglyphs and adversarial training as the defences. That is my UTS-39 skeleton + certificate, plus adversarial training — measured against each attack tier.

anomaly detection (future work)

Isolation Forest, One-Class SVM and Mahalanobis distance from the abnormality lecture are my named next step — an adversarial-input detector for obfuscated mail.

And the framing for drift comes from the same course: my 2008 model is Taleb’s turkey — confident right up to Thanksgiving. Relying on historical data to predict the future is exactly the inductive trap the abnormality lecture warned about.
